1. What Business Analysis Really Is (Mindset)

Business analysis is not documentation. It is structured thinking about how a business works and how it can improve.

At its core, every analysis answers three questions:

What is happening now? (AS-IS state)
What should happen? (TO-BE state)
Why is there a gap, and how do we close it?

A common mistake:

Beginners start with solutions.

Correct approach:

Start with understanding the system, then define the problem, then propose improvements.

2. The Universal Business System Model

Any business can be broken into 5 layers:

Market — where we operate
Competition — who already solves this problem
Company — what we are capable of
Customers — who we serve
Economics — how value is created and captured

If any layer is missing → analysis is incomplete.

3. How to Analyze Competitors (Practical Method)
3.1 What to collect for each competitor
Product structure and features
Pricing model
Target audience (segments)
Sales and distribution channels
User experience (UX)
Marketing channels and positioning
Customer feedback (especially negative reviews)
3.2 Competitive Analysis Matrix

Score each competitor:

Dimension	Score (1–5)	Notes
Price competitiveness		Expensive vs affordable
Product usability		UX simplicity and clarity
Speed / efficiency		Time-to-value
Brand trust		Reputation and credibility
Innovation		How modern and advanced
3.3 How to find real weaknesses

Look for:

Repeated customer complaints
Complex or slow processes
Overpricing vs perceived value
Product limitations or restrictions
Outdated UX patterns
Missing features in critical user journeys

👉 Key insight:
A competitor weakness = a potential market entry point.

4. How to Analyze Your Own Company

Break analysis into 4 dimensions:

4.1 Product
What do we offer?
Which problem do we solve?
Why is our solution better (or not)?
4.2 Operations
How efficient are internal processes?
Where are bottlenecks?
What can be automated?
4.3 Customers
Who actually uses the product (not just target persona)?
Where do users drop off?
Why do customers churn?
4.4 Financials
Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)
Lifetime Value (LTV)
Margins and profitability
Break-even structure
5. How to Identify Real Business Problems

A problem is real if:

It is repeated or systematic
It impacts time, revenue, or cost
Users or business already feel the pain
It can be measured

If none of these are true → it is not a problem, just an assumption or opinion.

6. How to Form Hypotheses

Use this structure:

If we do X → then Y will happen → because Z

Example:

If we simplify the checkout flow → conversion will increase → because users currently abandon at step 2 due to complexity.

Every hypothesis must be:

Testable
Measurable
Linked to a metric
7. Prioritization Framework

Use RICE:

Reach — how many users are affected
Impact — how strong the effect is
Confidence — certainty level
Effort — implementation cost

Formula:

(Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort

8. How to Draw Conclusions (Critical Skill)

Bad conclusion:

“Improve UX”

Good conclusion:

“Users drop off at the payment step due to a complex form. Simplifying it is expected to increase conversion by X%.”

A strong conclusion always includes:

Problem
Root cause
Measurable impact

10. Core Principle of a Business Analyst

A good analyst does not give ideas — they reduce uncertainty.

If you cannot:

measure the problem
explain the cause
estimate the impact

👉 then the analysis is incomplete.
